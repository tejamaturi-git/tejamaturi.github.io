Why Medicine

The personal experiences that shaped the decision to pursue a medical career.

My interest in medicine grew from years of observing the healthcare journeys of my grandfather and grandmother. My grandfather went through repeated cardiac procedures, emergency hospital visits, a temporary pacemaker, permanent pacemaker placement, valve replacement surgery, rehabilitation, and later treatment for stage IV mouth cancer. These experiences showed me the complexity of care, the importance of timely intervention, and the impact of compassionate clinicians.

My grandmother taught me the importance of prevention and screening. After persistent arm pain, a mammogram led to early detection of breast cancer. Her recovery reinforced that regular checkups and early diagnosis can change outcomes dramatically.

Together, their stories taught me that medicine is not only about treatment after illness appears; it is also about early detection, lifestyle change, prevention, and long-term support.

What I learned from my family

  • Emergency medicine can be life-saving when every minute matters.
  • Specialists, nurses, therapists, and families all contribute to recovery.
  • Preventive care and regular screenings detect disease earlier.
  • Healthy nutrition, walking, sunlight, and exercise matter for long-term wellness.
  • Medicine combines science, patience, and human connection.

“Some of the most important medical interventions happen long before a patient enters the hospital.”

My grandparents’ experiences showed me how regular checkups, mammograms, and early testing can detect disease earlier and improve outcomes.

This lesson reinforced my belief that good medicine includes screening, education, and preventive care.
